Chapter 5

Ideology-Based Argument Generation

Two approaches to generating audience-tailored arguments: a pre-LLM controlled synthesis based on rhetorical strategies, and an LLM-era rewriting method that transforms ineffective arguments into effective ones for a target ideology.

Generation

Overview

This chapter addresses the generation of arguments tailored to specific audiences. The two contributions reflect the technical landscape at the time of their development. The first, written before instruction-based LLMs were widely available, approximates audience-aware generation via rhetorical strategies (emotional vs. logical appeal) in a controlled setting. The second, developed in the LLM era, directly models audience ideology to rewrite ineffective arguments into effective ones.

Together, the contributions span a decade of argument generation research and demonstrate a clear progression — from constrained, strategy-driven synthesis to open-ended, audience-conditioned rewriting.

Contribution 1 — Rhetorical Strategy Based Generation

Research Question

RQ 3. How can arguments be generated to resonate with audiences affected by rhetorical strategies (i.e., emotional vs. logical appeal)?

Controlled generation setting: given a topic, stance, and pool of argumentative discourse units (ADUs), generate a pathos- or logos-oriented argument.

Addressed Gap

Prior argument generation was limited to short-span text or statistical synthesis, often producing incoherent multi-sentence arguments. This work introduces a systematic, three-step pipeline — abstract, select & arrange, phrase — that generates coherent arguments optimised for a target rhetorical strategy rather than surface-level fluency alone.

Key Contributions

  • A new view of argument synthesis combining argumentative and rhetorical considerations with language modelling over argumentative discourse units (ADUs).
  • A novel approach that clusters ADUs by style, learns to select and arrange them, and then phrases them to generate strategy-specific arguments for any topic and stance.
  • Experimental evidence that pathos- and logos-oriented arguments can be generated computationally and are distinguishable by annotators.

Bridging Contribution Datasets

Bridging Contribution — Ideology-Based Argument Datasets

Between the two main generation contributions sits a dataset construction effort that serves both. Drawing on a large-scale debate dataset, two ideology-labelled argument corpora were created — one for conservative audiences and one for liberal audiences — providing the empirical foundation for the LLM-era rewriting task.

Effectiveness labels are derived from pre- and post-debate audience votes, operationalizing whether an argument shifted a cross-ideological audience's perspective.

Datasets

Conservative Dataset

~82,000 arguments with binary effectiveness labels for conservative audiences

Contribution 2 — Effective Argument Rewriting

Research Question

RQ 4. How can arguments be effectively generated to resonate with audiences with specific beliefs?

Context Snapshot

IArguerLiberal / Conservative Americans
IIArgumentDebate Arguments · style transfer (primary focus)
IIIAudienceLiberal / Conservative Americans
IVEffectEffectiveness: ineffective → effective (binary)
Goal: rewrite an ineffective argument to be effective for a target ideology, retaining content while adjusting style.

Addressed Gap

While prior work tackled argument synthesis, quality assessment, and text style transfer in isolation, this work is the first to formulate and empirically study ideology-conditioned argument rewriting — adjusting style while preserving content to shift a cross-ideological audience's perspective.

Methodology

  • Data Curation. Conservative (~82k) and liberal (~55k) argument datasets with binary effectiveness labels derived from audience voting.
  • Generation. Three LLM-based methods with in-context learning: zero-shot prompting, few-shot prompting (with an effective argument as exemplar), and LLM steering via activation engineering.
  • Evaluation. Automated classifier, LLM-as-a-judge, and human evaluation of 100 argument tuples (1,240 human annotations total) for both ideologies.

Key Contributions

  • A new style transfer task: transform an ineffective argument into an effective one for a target ideology.
  • ~32,000 argument rewrites from 500 ineffective arguments per ideology.
  • Empirical evidence that both human annotators and LLM judges prefer the rewrites over the originals for both ideologies.
  • Models can successfully rewrite for one ideology while remaining resistant to the other.
