Chapter 4

The Role of Style in Audience Persuasion

Which non-topical linguistic features make an argument effective — and does that differ across political ideologies, ideology intensity, and personality profiles?

Analysis

Overview

After defining what makes an argument effective for different audience profiles (Chapter 3), this chapter asks how — specifically, which stylistic choices drive that effectiveness. Style is operationalized as non-topical text features that capture how something is said rather than what is said: emotional tone, subjectivity, argumentativeness, and related cues.

Using supervised machine learning, feature engineering, and inferential statistics, the chapter identifies which style features correlate with persuasive effectiveness for liberals vs. conservatives, lean vs. extreme ideologues, and different personality clusters — across three rhetorical canons: style (Elocutio), topic scope (Inventio), and discourse structure (Dispositio).

Key Findings

  • Style affects liberal but not conservative readers; extreme but not lean ideologues; and high-neuroticism readers but not balanced personality profiles.
  • News editorials on national topics have the highest stylistic persuasive effect — greater than state-level or international topics.
  • A negative emotional tone in the editorial body correlates with effectiveness for liberal audiences.
  • Style-oriented classifiers outperform content-oriented ones for predicting effectiveness for specific profiles.
